All About Sugar and the Keto Diet

There are two universal truths about sugar. One is that it is often delicious. The other is that it can be harmful to the body. If you are attempting to put your body into ketosis, you must steer clear of refined sugars.

How Refined Sugar Impacts Ketosis

Refined sugar, also known as granulated or table sugar, is a health disaster in general. It has long been linked to obesity, type 2 diabetes and heart disease. If you are following a ketogenic diet, it is certainly no use to you. Refined sugars are the first thing that need to go.

Refined sugar is made up entirely of simple carbohydrates, broken down by the body almost immediately. This provides a quick burst of carb-centric energy – the so-called, sugar rush inspired by sweet foods. Just as night follows day, however, a sugar rush is followed by a sugar crash. The energy provided by refined sugar burns brightly but briefly. The body is quickly left devoid of fuel as it has used up these carbs in the blink of an eye.

What is more, sugar is famously addictive. Eating sugar floods the brain with dopamine, so you will immediately crave more. What the heck, you may think, I’ve already started a cheat day, in for a penny in for a pound – I’ll have more sugar and get back on track tomorrow.

The flaw with this theory is that it can take up to three days to re-enter ketosis after consuming refined sugar. A stop-start relationship will confuse your body, forcing it to continually adapt to changes in diet. This leads to the infamous, keto flu. Furthermore, if you up your fat intake during this period, you may gain weight. Your body will not be burning off fat reserves as quickly as it would in full ketosis.

Are Natural Sugars Allowed on the Keto Diet?

If you are following the keto diet, you can still consume naturally occurring sugars. Fructose is the most common example. A handful of fruits are permitted, and even recommended, on the keto diet. Natural sugars are also found in lactose. This is important to note, as if you are following a keto meal plan, you are likely to consume your fair share of dairy products.

Natural sugars are not as dangerous for the body as their refined counterparts. They must still be consumed in moderation, though. Excessive natural sugars can still push the body out of ketosis. Use fructose and lactose to keep your cravings at bay and satisfy your sweet tooth so you do not fall off the wagon and end up devouring cakes and sweets. Just do not make them a cornerstone of your daily consumption.

The best way to gain sugars through lactose is cheese. If you enjoy cheese, you may wish to consider switching milk for a plant-based alternative. Unsweetened soy, almond or oat milk are all options. Any kind of cow’s milk, even if it is skimmed, is high in lactose and best avoided if you are also eating cheese.

When it comes to fructose, examine these panels to assess which fruits contain suitable levels of fructose, and which should be avoided.

Stick with this advice and your body should remain in ketosis, without subjecting yourself to any unpleasant cravings, or worse, withdrawal symptoms.

Are Artificial Sweeteners Keto-Friendly?

One of the great things about the keto diet is that you frequently will not need to sacrifice flavour in your food. The same goes for sugar, to an extent. As discussed, refined sugars are a no-fly zone if you are trying to get your body into a state of ketosis. You can make use of a range of artificial sweeteners, though.

Before we discuss what sweeteners you can use, let us clarify what remains off-limits. If you are looking to enter or retain ketosis, add the following to your list of no-no foods.

  • Honey. This is unfortunate, as high-quality honey contains many antioxidants. This is why some health-conscious people use it over sugar. Alas, even the best honey is still packed with enough carbohydrates to push your body out of ketosis.
  • Maple Syrup. Much like honey, this syrup has a range of positive micronutrients. It is even more densely packed with carbs, though.
  • Agave Nectar. A popular liquid sweetener, this sweetener contains so much concentrated fructose that your body will struggle to maintain a blood-sugar balance. This will impact ketosis.
  • Splenda. One of the biggest names in the artificial sweetener market, Splenda contains maltodextrin, a sweetener so processed that it virtually matches refined sugar for carbs. We do not recommend Splenda on the keto diet for this reason.

Having established what you cannot have, let us look at the artificial sweeteners that are considered keto-friendly. Thankfully, there are a handful to choose from, ensuring that anybody with a sweet tooth can still follow this diet plan:

  • Xylitol. Possibly the most popular sweetener of all, this substance tastes as sweet as sugar but does not impact blood sugar or insulin levels. Ergo, it can be considered the ideal sweetener for the keto diet.
  • Erythritol. This sweetener tastes almost the same as sugar, but it is virtually calorie-neutral and will not impact your carb count.
  • Stevia. Arguably the most natural artificial sweetener of all, extracted from the stevia rebaudiana plant. Stevia is virtually carb-free, so it is great for keto, but the taste is so intense that a little goes a long way. Do not replace sugar for stevia on a 1:1 ratio!

The keto diet does not mean that you need to quit sugar completely. You just need to moderate your intake and stick to naturally occurring sweet tastes as much as possible. Refined sugar is best avoided at the best of times. If you are seeking a state of ketosis, it must be cut out of your diet.
